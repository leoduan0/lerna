### Matrix-Matrix Multiplication

Two matrices can be multiplied iff the number of rows of the first matrix is equal to the number of columns of the second matrix. The resultant matrix has the same number of rows as the number of rows of the first matrix and the resultant matrix has the same number of columns as the number of columns of the second matrix. _i.e._:

$$ A_{a \times b} \times B_{b \times c} = C_{a \times c} $$

To multiply matrices:

1. First, multiply each of the elements of the first row of the first matrix with the first column of the second matrix correspondingly. This will correspond to the $$ (1, 1) $$ element of the resultant matrix.
1. Then, multiply each of the elements of the next row of the first matrix with the first column of the second matrix correspondingly. This will correspond to the $$ (n, 1) $$, where $$ n $$ is the row of the first matrix that is being processed.
1. Repeat step 2 until all the rows of the first matrix have been processed.
1. Then, repeat steps 2 to 3 for the next column of the second matrix until all columns of the second matrix have been processed.

<Block text="Example">

$$ \begin{bmatrix} a_{1,1} & a_{1,2} & a_{1,3} \\ a_{2,1} & a_{2,2} & a_{2,3} \end{bmatrix} \times \begin{bmatrix} b_{1,1} & b_{1,2} & b_{1,3} \\ b_{2,1} & b_{2,2} & b_{2,3} \\ b_{3,1} & b_{3,2} & b_{3,3} \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} a_{1,1}b_{1,1}+a_{1,2}b_{2,1}+a_{1,3}b_{3,1} & a_{1,1}b_{1,2}+a_{1,2}b_{2,2}+a_{1,3}b_{3,2} & a_{1,1}b_{1,3}+a_{1,2}b_{2,3}+a_{1,3}b_{3,3} \\ a_{2,1}b_{1,1}+a_{2,2}b_{2,1}+a_{2,3}b_{3,1} & a_{2,1}b_{1,2}+a_{2,2}b_{2,2}+a_{2,3}b_{3,2} & a_{2,1}b_{1,3}+a_{2,2}b_{2,3}+a_{2,3}b_{3,3} \end{bmatrix} $$

$$ \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 3 \\ 4 & 5 & 6 \end{bmatrix} \times \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 3 \\ 4 & 5 & 6 \\ 7 & 8 & 9 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \cdot 1+2 \cdot 4+3 \cdot 7 & 1 \cdot 2+2 \cdot 5+3 \cdot 8 & 1 \cdot 3+2 \cdot 6+3 \cdot 9 \\ 4 \cdot 1+5 \cdot 4+6 \cdot 7 & 4 \cdot 2+5 \cdot 5+6 \cdot 8 & 4 \cdot 3+5 \cdot 6+6 \cdot 9 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 30 & 36 & 42 \\ 66 & 81 & 96 \end{bmatrix} $$

</Block>

## Division

Matrices cannot be divided, but one could find a matrix's inverse and multiply it as an alternative.

## Transposition

A matrix can be transformed by, for all rows of the matrix, making the n-th row of the matrix become the nth column of the matrix:

<Rule
  statement={
    <MathExpression
      math='\begin{bmatrix} \textcolor{#FF0000}{a_{11}} & \textcolor{#FF0000}{a_{12}} & \textcolor{#FF0000}{a_{13}} \\ \textcolor{#F28500}{a_{21}} & \textcolor{#F28500}{a_{22}} & \textcolor{#F28500}{a_{23}} \\ \textcolor{#007FFF}{a_{31}} & \textcolor{#007FFF}{a_{32}} & \textcolor{#007FFF}{a_{33}} \end{bmatrix}^T = \begin{bmatrix} \textcolor{#FF0000}{a_{11}} & \textcolor{#F28500}{a_{21}} & \textcolor{#007FFF}{a_{31}} \\ \textcolor{#FF0000}{a_{12}} & \textcolor{#F28500}{a_{22}} & \textcolor{#007FFF}{a_{32}} \\ \textcolor{#FF0000}{a_{13}} & \textcolor{#F28500}{a_{23}} & \textcolor{#007FFF}{a_{33}} \end{bmatrix}'

      type="block"
    />
  }
/>

<Block text="Example">

$$ {\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 3 \\ 4 & 5 & 6 \\ 7 & 8 & 9 \end{bmatrix}}^T = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 4 & 7 \\ 2 & 5 & 8 \\ 3 & 6 & 9 \end{bmatrix} $$

</Block>
