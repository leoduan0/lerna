# Definite Integration

To find the area of an area bounded by the x-axis, lines $$ x=a $$ and $$ x=b $$, and the function $$ f(x) $$ (commonly referred to as "the area under the function"), **definite integration** can be used.

Definite integration is identical to indefinite integration except that we will introduce an **upper bound** and a **lower bound**. Then, using the **fundamental theorem of calculus**, we can evaluate a definite integral.

Given $$ \int_a^b f(x) \,dx $$, $$ a $$ is the lower bound and $$ b $$ is the upper bound.

The fundamental theorem of calculus is as follows:

<Rule
  statement={
    <Math math={'\\int_a^b f(x) \\,dx = F(b) - F(a)'} type={'block'} />
  }
  terms={[
    {
      term: 'a',
      annotation: <span>Lower bound</span>,
    },
    {
      term: 'b',
      annotation: <span>Upper bound</span>,
    },
    {
      term: 'F(b)',
      annotation: (
        <span>
          <Math math={'f(x)'} type={'inline'} /> integrated at{' '}
          <Math math={'b'} type={'inline'} />.
        </span>
      ),
    },
    {
      term: 'F(a)',
      annotation: (
        <span>
          <Math math={'f(x)'} type={'inline'} /> integrated at{' '}
          <Math math={'a'} type={'inline'} />.
        </span>
      ),
    },
  ]}
/>

<Example>

| Procedure                                                                          | Annotation                                                                                                                         |
| ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| $$ \int_1^5 x^3 \,dx $$                                                            | The evaluated form of this integral would give us the area bounded by constraints $$ x=1 $$, $$ x=5 $$, $$ x^3 $$, and the x-axis. |
| $$ \because f(x) = x^3 $$ $$ \therefore F(x) = \frac{x^4}{4} $$                    | Find $$ F(x) $$.                                                                                                                   |
| $$ \int_1^5 x^3 \,dx = \left. \frac{x^4}{4} \right\vert_1^5 = F(5) - F(1) = 156 $$ | Apply the fundamental theorem of calculus, and we arrive at our answer.                                                            |

</Example>

## Derivative of Definite Integrals

Given a function $$ f(x) = \int_{a(x)}^{b(x)} g(x) \,dx $$, its derivative is given by the following.

<Rule
  statement={
    <Math
      math={
        'f^{\\prime}(x) = \\frac{d}{dx} \\int_{a(x)}^{b(x)} g(x) \\,dx = g(b(x)) \\cdot b^{\\prime}(x) - g(a(x)) \\cdot a^{\\prime}(x)'
      }
      type={'block'}
    />
  }
/>
